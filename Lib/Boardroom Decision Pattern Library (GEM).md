# **Boardroom Decision Pattern Library v1.0: Enterprise Pre-Decision Clarity Layer**

## **Executive Synthesis: The Structural Anatomy of Boardroom Decisions**

Corporate governance at the highest levels of the enterprise is increasingly threatened by a crisis of information density. Executives and board directors are routinely inundated with exhaustive pre-read materials, lengthy slide decks, and operational memos prior to high-stakes meetings. However, these materials systematically fail to structure the underlying decision logic, defaulting instead to chronological storytelling and additive complexity1. This phenomenon generates "Certainty Theater"—the performance of analytical rigor using legacy metrics and linear projections that mask invariant physical constraints and structural risks. When management teams present chronological narratives, they often guide the board toward a single, predetermined binary choice, effectively circumventing the board’s fiduciary duty of independent, multi-dimensional judgment.  
The BoardroomVoiceAgent functions as an enterprise "Pre-Decision Clarity Layer." It is distinctly not a generic text summarizer; rather, it is an epistemic scaffolding engine designed to deconstruct complex corporate narratives and isolate the invariant logic of a decision3. By stripping away narrative noise, the agent identifies the hidden business assumptions anchoring the enterprise to past successes, isolates the specific empirical evidence that alters the current operational baseline, and maps the scenario across materially distinct options1. This methodology is directly aligned with the highest standards of corporate governance, including the G20/OECD Principles of Corporate Governance, the COSO Enterprise Risk Management Framework, and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Thailand's Corporate Governance Code for Listed Companies 20174.  
Advanced corporate legal frameworks demand rigorous epistemic hygiene from directors. For instance, under Section 85 of the Thai Public Limited Companies Act B.E. 2535 (1992), directors are legally mandated to perform their duties with integrity, honesty, and due care to preserve the interests of the company7. Failure to exercise this fiduciary duty—especially when authorizing major capital expansions, related-party transactions, or safety-critical operational changes—can result in severe civil and criminal liabilities, including shareholder derivative lawsuits9. Therefore, the boardroom decision must be elevated from a mere approval of management's chronological story into an act of "Sovereign Intent," where the board explicitly acknowledges the unavoidable trade-offs and structural risks they are authorizing3.  
To facilitate this standard of care, the Boardroom Decision Pattern Library standardizes the logical deconstruction of 14 recurring executive decision archetypes. Every pattern strictly identifies the boundaries of accountability, the variables that genuinely shift the baseline, and the exact minimum evidence required before a decision can be safely made. Crucially, this library introduces the mechanism of "Semantic Backpressure." When a proposal contains logic leaks, split-brain contradictions (e.g., demanding aggressive top-line growth while systematically cutting the maintenance budget of safety-critical physical assets), or relies on human vigilance to override invariant physics, the agent applies Semantic Backpressure1. This halts the automated progression of the briefing, flags the structural contradiction, and formulates precise, executive-ready meeting room questions that directors must resolve to satisfy their fiduciary oversight obligations.

For an immediate, high-impact enterprise implementation, the BoardroomVoiceAgent should be deployed using a Minimum Viable Pattern Set of eight core archetypes. This specific selection ensures balanced coverage across the most frequent and highest-risk domains encountered by executive committees, providing immediate stabilization of capital allocation and risk oversight functions3.

## **The Boardroom Decision Pattern Library**

The following 14 decision archetypes represent the exhaustive structural logic required to brief corporate directors safely and effectively. They are strictly categorized to separate procedural guidance from domain-specific facts, ensuring that the BoardroomVoiceAgent never prescribes a preferred strategic option, but rather illuminates the structural physics of the choice1.

### **Strategy and Portfolio Choices**

The allocation of strategic resources and the rationalization of legacy portfolios are consistently undermined by base-rate neglect and sunk-cost fallacies. Directors must navigate the tension between defending highly profitable but structurally declining core businesses and pivoting toward speculative, high-growth adjacencies. The following patterns structure the epistemic boundaries necessary to avoid ego-driven capability assumptions and to enforce objective sovereign advantage audits3.

| Pattern Field | BDP-STRAT-01: Sovereign Strategic Pivot (Core-Adjacent Horizon Choice) |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Pattern ID** | BDP-STRAT-01 |
| **Pattern Name** | Sovereign Strategic Pivot (Core-Adjacent Horizon Choice) |
| **Business Context** | Allocating strategic resources between expanding/defending a highly profitable but declining core business and entering high-growth, speculative adjacent business models under extreme uncertainty. |
| **Typical Executive Forum** | Board of Directors / Strategic Review Committee |
| **Decision Trigger Signals** | Core business revenue/profit decline rate exceeding 8% YoY; major competitor entering adjacent market with a validated 'green premium'; formal management proposal to launch a speculative venture. |
| **Informational Signals (Non-Decision)** | Routine quarterly macroeconomic forecasts; generic competitor marketing announcements; high-level market size (TAM) charts without localized acquisition friction modeling. |
| **Common Prior-Assumption Archetypes** | Legacy Mastery Assumption (belief that core operational excellence guarantees adjacent success); Lower-Risk Core Assumption (belief that maintaining capital in a declining core is inherently less risky). |
| **Variables Changing Baseline** | Capital availability (WACC shifts); landed Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) in adjacent space; regulatory carbon tax or subsidy thresholds; internal technical skill readiness index. |
| **Typical Material Options** | 1\. **Core Defender:** Maintain capital strictly within the core, optimizing costs and using surplus cash for share buybacks. 2\. **Real Option Exploration:** Establish small-scale joint ventures or pilots with strict stop-losses. 3\. **Sovereign Strategic Pivot:** Commit major strategic capital to build a market-leading position in the new vertical. |
| **Unavoidable Trade-off Structure** | Immediate EPS preservation and cash retention (Core Defender) vs. Long-term strategic relevance and survival under technological obsolescence (Sovereign Strategic Pivot). |
| **Minimum Evidence Categories** | Decay rate of core cash flows; landed CAC friction coefficients; regulatory green-premium validity timelines; Sovereign Capability Audit mapping transferable legal IP and assets. |
| **Common Missing-Evidence Indicators** | Presentation of adjacent Total Addressable Market (TAM) without corresponding customer acquisition cost (CAC) calculations; lack of scenario-based stress-tests for core business decline. |
| **Accountability Roles to Clarify** | Chief Executive Officer (Sovereign Decision Maker); Chief Strategy Officer (Formulator and Capability Auditor); Business Unit Leaders (Operational Execution Owners). |
| **Semantic Backpressure Conditions** | If forced to make a multi-million-dollar commitment based on industry trend slide decks without a verified Sovereign Capability Audit; if the pivot requires reducing core maintenance below physical safety limits. |
| **Decision Safe Now** | Allocation of a bounded seed budget (\<5% of annual CapEx) to fund feasibility studies, technical due diligence, and small-scale pilot options. |
| **Decision Not Yet Safe** | Entering multi-year take-or-pay commercial agreements or initiating full physical shutdown of profitable core facilities. |
| **Meeting Room Questions** | 1\. "What unique, non-replicable sovereign advantage do we possess in this adjacent space, or are we paying a massive premium just to learn a competitor's baseline business?" 2\. "At what specific decay rate of our core business cash flows does doing nothing become a higher-risk option than this speculative pivot?" |
| **One-Line Decision Cue** | *"To capture growth in \[Adjacent Market\], we authorize \[Resource Allocation\] for \[Phase-1 Option\], maintaining \[Core Defense\] until \[Reality Trigger\] is met."* |
| **Common Reasoning Failures** | Base-rate neglect (ignoring high statistical failure rates of corporate adjacent entries); Additive Bias (believing adjacent products can be tacked onto current teams with zero training). |
| **Prohibited Inference/Overreach** | Declaring any adjacent market trend as a guaranteed future state; prescribing a single 'winner' among strategic options. |
| **Authoritative Sources** | Thai SEC Corporate Governance Code 2017 (Principle 2); G20/OECD Principles of Corporate Governance. |
| **Confidence Level** | High (Multi-Frame Reality Check Verified) |

| **Pattern Name** | Portfolio Rationalization & Asset Divestment (The Sunk-Cost Liberation) |
| **Business Context** | Sunsetting or divesting an underperforming, legacy business unit or physical asset that consumes disproportionate executive attention and capital relative to its systemic contribution. |
| **Typical Executive Forum** | Board of Directors / Audit and Finance Committee |
| **Decision Trigger Signals** | Legacy business unit consuming \>30% of executive bandwidth while producing \<5% of net income; physical asset operating permanently below cash-breakeven; unsolicited acquisition bids. |
| **Informational Signals (Non-Decision)** | Routine monthly operational turnaround performance updates; updated asset depreciation schedules without cash flow impact; generic competitor restructuring announcements. |
| **Common Prior-Assumption Archetypes** | Reputation Preservation Dogma (sustaining loss-making units protects brand value); Book-Value Floor Myth (historical CapEx is a value floor that must be recovered before exiting). |
| **Variables Changing Baseline** | Asset secondary market liquidity index; opportunity cost of executive attention; environmental decommissioning liability estimates (Asset Retirement Obligations); union contract constraints. |
| **Typical Material Options** | 1\. **Turnaround Continuation:** Proceed with existing restructuring plan, injecting incremental capital to achieve cost-parity. 2\. **Strategic Carve-out & Divestment:** Initiate a formal transaction to divest the asset, accepting immediate write-downs. 3\. **Controlled Wind-down:** Halt operations, write off remaining book value, and decommission the physical site. |
| **Unavoidable Trade-off Structure** | Immediate non-cash balance sheet write-down and potential negative market reaction vs. Ongoing cash bleed and dilution of executive focus. |
| **Minimum Evidence Categories** | Daily cash burn rate of the asset; opportunity cost audit of management bandwidth; third-party environmental liability assessment (ARO pricing); detailed employee severance models. |
| **Common Missing-Evidence Indicators** | Lack of Net Present Value (NPV) comparisons between divestment and turnaround under negative cost-of-capital assumptions; absence of formal third-party asset valuation benchmarks. |
| **Accountability Roles to Clarify** | Chief Financial Officer (Transaction and Valuation Lead); Head of Human Resources (Severance and Exit Compliance); General Counsel (Liability Boundary Custodian). |
| **Semantic Backpressure Conditions** | If requested to approve 'bridge capital' to a turnaround plan without a predefined, hard stop-loss trigger; if divestment proposals hide Asset Retirement Obligations in accounting footnotes. |
| **Decision Safe Now** | Structuring a formal, non-binding expression-of-interest (EOI) process to gauge external market appetite and obtain real valuation bids. |
| **Decision Not Yet Safe** | Entering into binding sale-and-purchase agreements (SPA) without a comprehensive environmental liability carve-out audit. |
| **Meeting Room Questions** | 1\. "If we were not already in this business today, would we write a check for the current written-down value of these assets to enter it now?" 2\. "What is the precise thermodynamic cost of carrying this asset—specifically, what strategic initiatives are we delaying because our best talent is trapped in this turnaround?" |
| **One-Line Decision Cue** | *"To liberate strategic bandwidth, we authorize \[Divestment/Wind-down\] of \[Asset/Unit\], recognizing a \[Write-down Size\] write-down to avoid \[Annual Cash Bleed\]."* |
| **Common Reasoning Failures** | Sunk-cost fallacy (clinging to legacy assets to justify past CapEx); Ego preservation (refusing to admit a strategic initiative has failed). |
| **Prohibited Inference/Overreach** | Declaring an asset structurally dead based solely on a cyclical market downturn; recommending specific transaction buyers. |
| **Authoritative Sources** | McKinsey Research on Active Portfolio Management; Harvard Business Review studies on Sunk-Cost Bias in Boardrooms. |
| **Confidence Level** | High (Logic Scrubbed) |

### **Capital Allocation and Investment**

The adjudication of sustaining capital expenditures and merger and acquisition (M\&A) valuations requires boards to look beyond idealized financial models and demand physical, forensic validation. Heavy industrial assets undergo thermodynamic degradation that cannot be captured by linear depreciation schedules, while acquisition premiums are routinely justified by aggressive, frictionless synergy assumptions that ignore integration realities3.

| Pattern Field | BDP-CAP-01: Strategic Asset Reinvestment (The Defend-vs-Retire Dilemma) |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Pattern ID** | BDP-CAP-01 |
| **Pattern Name** | Strategic Asset Reinvestment (The Defend-vs-Retire Dilemma) |
| **Business Context** | Deciding whether to reinvest major sustaining capital (CapEx) to maintain, upgrade, or retrofit an aging, heavy industrial physical plant or core technological asset versus decommissioning it. |
| **Typical Executive Forum** | Investment Committee / Capital Allocation Board |
| **Decision Trigger Signals** | Asset requiring a capital overhaul \>30% of replacement value; reliability degradation causing unplanned outages \>15% of operational time; mandatory regulatory compliance deadlines. |
| **Informational Signals (Non-Decision)** | Routine maintenance CapEx approvals; unplanned downtime reports without life-cycle analysis; software vendor minor upgrade notifications. |
| **Common Prior-Assumption Archetypes** | Cheap Legacy Dogma (maintaining a legacy asset is always cheaper than building a modern replacement); Constant Value Assumption (the asset's strategic role remains unchanged despite market evolution). |
| **Variables Changing Baseline** | Asset physical degradation index; shadow price of carbon/emissions penalties; alternative technology efficiency curves; stranded cost write-off thresholds. |
| **Typical Material Options** | 1\. **Defend and Modernize:** Approve full capital overhaul, retrofitting the asset to extend useful life by 10+ years. 2\. **Sweat the Asset:** Limit capital spend to mandatory safety requirements, running under a degraded regime with a hard retirement date. 3\. **Retire and Transition:** Decommission the asset immediately and transition to modern or third-party alternatives. |
| **Unavoidable Trade-off Structure** | Immediate high CapEx exposure and construction downtime (Modernize) vs. Long-term operational vulnerability, margin erosion, and safety risk (Sweat). |
| **Minimum Evidence Categories** | Physical asset forensic degradation audit (non-destructive testing); long-term OpEx delta of legacy vs. modern; value of production downtime during transition; regulatory compliance risk index. |
| **Common Missing-Evidence Indicators** | Lack of alternative-technology NPV comparisons; omission of transition and decommissioning costs; reliability projections based on historical rather than forensic wear models. |
| **Accountability Roles to Clarify** | Asset Owner / Plant Manager (Operational Truth Custodian); Chief Financial Officer (Capital Efficiency); Chief Sustainability Officer (Regulatory Risk). |
| **Semantic Backpressure Conditions** | If a major modernization budget is requested without a detailed forensic wear-and-tear audit; if economics assume constant raw material pricing ignoring long-term transition trends. |
| **Decision Safe Now** | Approval of immediate engineering and design study budgets (FEED) to precisely cost modernization versus replacement options. |
| **Decision Not Yet Safe** | Signing long-term asset modernization contracts or ordering long-lead equipment before FEED results are validated. |
| **Meeting Room Questions** | 1\. "Are we investing capital to make this asset competitive for another decade, or are we paying a massive premium just to delay its inevitable obsolescence?" 2\. "What is the Net Present Value of shutting this asset down today and outsourcing its capacity, versus the fully-loaded cost of this modernization?" |
| **One-Line Decision Cue** | *"We approve \[CapEx Allocation\] for \[Modernize vs. Sweat\] on \[Asset Name\], maintaining \[Operating Limits\] until \[Transition Milestone\] is achieved."* |
| **Common Reasoning Failures** | Certainty Theater (using single-point reliability projections); ignoring cumulative OpEx of a degrading asset versus upfront CapEx of a modern one. |
| **Prohibited Inference/Overreach** | Declaring an industrial asset structurally unsafe without official engineering audit evidence; prescribing specific engineering vendor contracts. |
| **Authoritative Sources** | COSO Enterprise Risk Management Framework; Real Options Theory (Dixit & Pindyck). |
| **Confidence Level** | High (Physical Calibration) |

| :---- | :---- |
| **Pattern ID** | BDP-CAP-02 |
| **Pattern Name** | Mergers & Acquisitions Valuation (The Synergistic Premium Audit) |
| **Business Context** | Evaluating a major acquisition target that promises rapid strategic/revenue growth and operational synergies but at a high valuation premium that risks overpaying. |
| **Typical Executive Forum** | Board of Directors / Investment Committee |
| **Decision Trigger Signals** | Due diligence findings showing target valuation requires \>40% synergy capture to justify the premium; competitive bidding pushing premiums \>30% above unaffected share price; major integration red flags. |
| **Informational Signals (Non-Decision)** | High-level target teaser decks; standard investment banker valuation models without synergy stress-tests; general industry consolidation news. |
| **Common Prior-Assumption Archetypes** | Frictionless Integration Myth (buyer can capture 100% of synergies within 12 months); Zero Churn Assumption (customer and employee retention will remain at 100% despite integration friction). |
| **Variables Changing Baseline** | Integration cycle time and friction coefficient; retention rate of target's key talent/customers; Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) of combined entity; antitrust review timelines. |
| **Typical Material Options** | 1\. **Proceed at Current Bid:** Approve transaction at the negotiated premium, accepting integration risks. 2\. **Structure Contingent Consideration:** Restructure the valuation, tying consideration to post-close synergy and performance milestones (Earn-out). 3\. **Terminate Negotiations:** Cease negotiations and exit the transaction to preserve cash. |
| **Unavoidable Trade-off Structure** | Rapid strategic expansion and market share acceleration vs. Severe earnings dilution, leverage risk, and high integration execution friction. |
| **Minimum Evidence Categories** | Probability-weighted synergy breakdown; talent and customer retention cost models; detailed regulatory/antitrust risk assessment; combined balance sheet debt-capacity stress-tests. |
| **Common Missing-Evidence Indicators** | Lack of probability-weighted synergy models (presenting only best-case scenarios); omission of detailed customer churn projections; absence of an explicit integration budget. |
| **Accountability Roles to Clarify** | Chief Executive Officer (Deal Sponsor); Chief Financial Officer (Valuation Custodian); Head of Integration (Post-close Execution Owner). |
| **Semantic Backpressure Conditions** | If 'strategic fit' is used to justify a premium unsupported by cash flow models; if legal or operational red-flags from due diligence are diluted in the final board deck. |
| **Decision Safe Now** | Approving a revised, non-binding Letter of Intent (LOI) that makes the transaction contingent on restructuring the valuation to include earn-outs. |
| **Decision Not Yet Safe** | Releasing the board’s final, unconditional vote of approval for the acquisition before securing regulatory/antitrust clearances. |
| **Meeting Room Questions** | 1\. "If we fail to capture a single dollar of cost synergies, does this acquisition still meet our minimum hurdle rate of return?" 2\. "Are we buying this asset because it has unique, non-replicable capabilities, or are we overpaying simply to prevent a competitor from acquiring it?" |
| **One-Line Decision Cue** | *"Authorize acquisition of \[Target\] at maximum price \[Value\], making \[X\]% of consideration contingent on \[Synergy Milestone\] verification."* |
| **Common Reasoning Failures** | Winner's curse (overpaying in a competitive bidding environment); optimism bias regarding integration speed and capability transfer. |
| **Prohibited Inference/Overreach** | Dictating the 'correct' valuation multiplier; declaring target management incompetent without audited evidence. |
| **Authoritative Sources** | Thai Public Limited Companies Act B.E. 2535 (Section 85); BCG research on M\&A transaction failure rates. |
| **Confidence Level** | High (Regulatory Fit) |

### **Growth, Margin, and Commercial Trade-offs**

Commercial pricing strategies and vertically integrated value realizations frequently fall victim to Goodhart’s Law, where localized sales targets or subsidiary profit margins cannibalize the consolidated group's economic value. Boards must structuralize pricing elasticity and multi-entity transfer pricing to prevent value leakage and ensure compliance with stringent global tax regulations3.

| Pattern Field | BDP-GROW-01: Commercial Pricing & Price Elasticity (Margin Preservation vs. Volume Retention) |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Pattern ID** | BDP-GROW-01 |
| **Pattern Name** | Commercial Pricing & Price Elasticity (Margin Preservation vs. Volume Retention) |
| **Business Context** | Managing commercial pricing strategy under market cost pressure (inflation) or competitive attacks, balancing unit margin against sales volume. |
| **Typical Executive Forum** | Executive Committee / Commercial Planning Board |
| **Decision Trigger Signals** | Raw material inflation eroding operating margins by \>150 basis points; competitor price cutting targeting core accounts; slowing volume growth (\<2%) with a stable market share. |
| **Informational Signals (Non-Decision)** | Monthly sales target trackers without margin indicators; general customer satisfaction surveys; macro commodity trends lacking local supply-chain impact modeling. |
| **Common Prior-Assumption Archetypes** | Inelastic Demand Myth (customers possess zero switching capacity and will absorb all price increases); Volume-at-all-Costs Dogma (market share is the only true proxy for survival). |
| **Variables Changing Baseline** | Customer price elasticity coefficient; switching costs and alternative supplier capacity; direct manufacturing variable cost margin; capacity utilization thresholds of production facilities. |
| **Typical Material Options** | 1\. **Preserve Margin via Price Hikes:** Implement immediate price increase to offset inflation, accepting volume erosion risk. 2\. **Defend Volume via Price Matching:** Maintain/lower pricing to match promotions, absorbing margin compression. 3\. **Value-Based Tiering:** Introduce lower-cost alternative product formulations to migrate price-sensitive customers. |
| **Unavoidable Trade-off Structure** | Immediate EBITDA margin percentage preservation (Price Hike) vs. Customer relationship longevity and plant capacity utilization (Price Matching). |
| **Minimum Evidence Categories** | Empirical price elasticity of demand models by customer segment; competitor cost-curve position; minimum operational run-rate of plants to avoid fixed-cost absorption penalties; switching friction index. |
| **Common Missing-Evidence Indicators** | Lack of volume-impact simulations under different scenarios; using generic historical elasticity figures rather than recent transactional data; no analysis of fixed-cost absorption impact on unit margins. |
| **Accountability Roles to Clarify** | Chief Commercial Officer (Volume/Pricing Execution); Chief Financial Officer (Margin/Fixed-Cost Auditor); Head of Manufacturing (Capacity Utilization Custodian). |
| **Semantic Backpressure Conditions** | If management proposes a major price hike without simulating the resulting volume decline and its fixed-cost absorption penalty on manufacturing units. |
| **Decision Safe Now** | Approval of localized, bounded price-elasticity testing (e.g., in a single geographic market or product tier) for a 30-day period. |
| **Decision Not Yet Safe** | Enforcing a global, permanent price increase across all enterprise accounts without a validated contract-by-contract risk assessment. |
| **Meeting Room Questions** | 1\. "At what volume erosion threshold does the margin gain from this price increase get completely wiped out by fixed-cost absorption penalties in our plants?" 2\. "Are we discounting our products because the market has structurally changed, or because our sales team is using price as a substitute for active selling?" |
| **One-Line Decision Cue** | *"To manage raw material inflation, we authorize a \[Price Hike\]% price adjustment on \[Product Segment\], accepting up to \[Volume Limit\]% volume loss."* |
| **Common Reasoning Failures** | Goodhart's Law (sales teams chasing volume targets through margin-killing discounts); ignoring capacity-utilization cliffs in manufacturing plants. |
| **Prohibited Inference/Overreach** | Declaring a competitor's pricing strategy illegal (e.g., predatory pricing) without a formal antitrust filing. |
| **Authoritative Sources** | Porter's Competitive Strategy (Cost Leadership vs. Differentiation); Academic literature on Corporate Pricing Power and Elasticity. |
| **Confidence Level** | High (Commercial Logic) |

| Pattern Field | BDP-GROW-02: Vertically Integrated Value Realization (The Multi-Entity Transfer Pricing Alignment) |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Pattern ID** | BDP-GROW-02 |
| **Pattern Name** | Vertically Integrated Value Realization (The Multi-Entity Transfer Pricing Alignment) |
| **Business Context** | Structuring transfer pricing and margin sharing in vertically integrated business models to optimize the total net integrated spread (NIS) rather than sub-optimizing at individual entity levels. |
| **Typical Executive Forum** | Board Audit Committee / Joint Finance Board |
| **Decision Trigger Signals** | Tax authority audit challenging market-conformity of cross-border transfer prices; silo-EBITDA incentives causing upstream units to starve downstream units of critical feedstock; downstream paper losses. |
| **Informational Signals (Non-Decision)** | Routine group tax filing submissions; silo-level monthly profit and loss statements without consolidation adjustment logs; general market index pricing feeds. |
| **Common Prior-Assumption Archetypes** | Spot-Index Fallacy (external market spot indices are the only fair benchmark for internal transfers); Autonomy Dogma (each subsidiary must maximize its own net profit, ignoring integrated profitability). |
| **Variables Changing Baseline** | Physical pipeline connectivity and switching cost between units; landed variable cost of upstream production; global tax differential rates across jurisdictions; local transfer pricing legal safe harbors. |
| **Typical Material Options** | 1\. **Strict Market-Parity Pricing:** Enforce external market spot-price indices for all internal transfers. 2\. **Cost-Plus Transfer Pricing with Shared Margin:** Utilize a cost-plus mechanism with a shared-margin formula to align incentives. 3\. **Net Integrated Spread (NIS) Profit Allocation:** Value the entire value chain as a single economic unit, distributing NIS based on physical capital allocation. |
| **Unavoidable Trade-off Structure** | Silo-level P\&L transparency and corporate legal separation compliance vs. Maximization of consolidated group net cash flow. |
| **Minimum Evidence Categories** | Landed variable cost of molecules across the entire chain; independent market pricing studies; local tax regulatory transfer pricing compliance guidelines; inter-company contract legal structures. |
| **Common Missing-Evidence Indicators** | Lack of integrated well-to-customer consolidated margin analysis; over-reliance on external benchmarks without adjusting for local logistics; no transfer pricing tax risk simulation. |
| **Accountability Roles to Clarify** | Group Chief Financial Officer (Consolidation Architect); Tax Director (Compliance Custodian); Business Unit Executives (Performance Evaluation Alignment). |
| **Semantic Backpressure Conditions** | If a proposal suggests divesting an unprofitable downstream unit without modeling the corresponding profit destruction in the upstream unit; if transfer prices are manipulated violating tax compliance. |
| **Decision Safe Now** | Adopting a dual-book reporting system (Silo P\&L for tax compliance, Net Integrated Spread for strategic capital allocation). |
| **Decision Not Yet Safe** | Implementing a permanent change to inter-company legal contracts and tax-filed transfer pricing methodologies before receiving a formal advance pricing agreement (APA) from tax regulators. |
| **Meeting Room Questions** | 1\. "Are we actually losing money on this downstream molecule, or are we simply shifting our corporate profits to another tax jurisdiction through an accounting fiction?" 2\. "How does our consolidated group profitability change if we stop running this downstream plant and sell the upstream raw material directly to the merchant market?" |
| **One-Line Decision Cue** | *"Apply \[Net Integrated Spread\] allocation to \[Group Units\], maintaining \[Silo-Tax Compliance\] books to optimize \[Group Cash Flow\]."* |
| **Common Reasoning Failures** | Silo sub-optimization (maximizing individual business unit performance at the expense of the overall group); Goodhart's Law in transfer pricing incentives. |
| **Prohibited Inference/Overreach** | Declaring transfer prices illegal; recommending aggressive tax-evasion transfer strategies. |
| **Authoritative Sources** | OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises and Tax Administrations; Section B.E. 2561 of the Thai Revenue Department Transfer Pricing Act. |
| **Confidence Level** | High (Regulatory Compliance) |

### **Operations, Resilience, and Safety**

Operational governance represents the hard boundary between business risk and physical consequence. Boards must continually arbitrate the tension between hyper-efficient production metrics and the thermodynamic reality of systemic disruption and catastrophic engineering failure3.

| Pattern Field | BDP-OPS-01: Safety-Critical Process Stand-down (The Operational Halt vs. Flow Resilience) |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Pattern ID** | BDP-OPS-01 |
| **Pattern Name** | Safety-Critical Process Stand-down (The Operational Halt vs. Flow Resilience) |
| **Business Context** | Managing operational safety thresholds or environmental risks that threaten to exceed acceptable levels, requiring a choice between an immediate operational shutdown and running in a degraded mode. |
| **Typical Executive Forum** | Board Risk & Safety Committee / Emergency Executive Board |
| **Decision Trigger Signals** | Safety instrumentation reporting metrics approaching catastrophic physical limits (LOPA Level 3/4); unresolved chronic process safety anomalies; regulatory enforcement notice alleging material threat to human life. |
| **Informational Signals (Non-Decision)** | Routine occupational safety reports (LTI, slips, trips); general facility wear-and-tear maintenance backlogs that do not affect containment or structural integrity. |
| **Common Prior-Assumption Archetypes** | Vigilance Dogma (administrative controls and worker vigilance are sufficient to mitigate physical process safety risks); Zero-Sum Shut-down (a shutdown is a binary choice between zero revenue and maximum safety). |
| **Variables Changing Baseline** | Physical plant operating envelope pressure, temperature, and corrosion levels; risk reduction factor (RRF) of safety-instrumented systems (SIS); regulatory legal liability under criminal environmental laws. |
| **Typical Material Options** | 1\. **Immediate Controlled Shutdown:** Initiate an immediate, controlled operational shutdown to perform engineering remediation. 2\. **Degraded Mode with Mitigations:** Continue operations at reduced throughput/pressure with continuous physical monitoring. 3\. **Bypass and Physical Risk Transfer:** Bypass faulty instrumentation and continue at full capacity using administrative risk-management procedures. |
| **Unavoidable Trade-off Structure** | Immediate high financial loss (shutting down, customer delivery penalties) vs. Potential catastrophic failure (loss of human life, environmental destruction, immediate revocation of License to Operate). |
| **Minimum Evidence Categories** | LOPA (Layer of Protection Analysis) risk reduction scores; forensic engineering analysis of physical wear/containment; cost of controlled shutdown vs. cost of catastrophic failure; legal opinion on director liability. |
| **Common Missing-Evidence Indicators** | Presenting 'worker awareness' or 'enhanced PPE' as primary mitigations for physical containment failures; lack of quantitative risk assessment (QRA) modeling showing catastrophic boundary probabilities. |
| **Accountability Roles to Clarify** | Plant Manager / Site Leader (First Line \- Operational Truth); Chief Safety / SHE Officer (Independent Auditor); Chief Executive Officer (Accountable Sovereign for Operational Continuation). |
| **Semantic Backpressure Conditions** | If management proposes bypassing engineering controls for a process safety hazard solely to meet production volume targets; if a safety proposal relies on administrative procedures to control LOPA Level 3 risks. |
| **Decision Safe Now** | Immediate implementation of an operational safety stand-down or degraded-capacity mode to perform high-resolution non-destructive testing (NDT) of critical pipes/vessels. |
| **Decision Not Yet Safe** | Authorizing full-capacity startup of decommissioned/repaired lines before an independent, third-party certified safety review is completed. |

## **Intentionally Excluded Archetypes and Anti-Patterns**

To preserve the epistemic rigor of BoardroomVoiceAgent and maintain strict alignment with structural governance principles—such as the fiduciary duties enshrined in Section 85 of the Thai Public Limited Companies Act—certain commonly requested "patterns" have been deliberately excluded from this library3.  
The BoardroomVoiceAgent must not process **Generic Financial Forecasting or Ad-hoc Capital Expenditure Requests**. Generalized financial forecasting inherently lacks physical constraint modeling and is therefore highly susceptible to "Certainty Theater," wherein manipulated spreadsheet variables secure arbitrary board approval3. True capital expenditure is governed by specific physical constraints and is thus mapped through *BDP-CAP-01 (Asset Reinvestment)* and *BDP-CAP-02 (M\&A Valuation)*.  
Similarly, **Corporate Culture Realignment** initiatives have been excluded. Cultural transformations are excessively subjective, lack concrete, enforceable material options, and do not present clear structural boundaries3. Attempts to systematize "culture" usually degrade into consulting-generic language. The mechanical constraints of culture are instead addressed through the explicit accountability structures defined in *BDP-ORG-01 (Operating Model Restructuring)*.  
**Generic Market Expansion and Geographic Launches** are excluded as they are excessively vague and lack invariant physics. Geographic launches frequently disguise fundamental capability gaps behind market excitement3. To ensure rigorous analysis, these decisions are forced through the constraints of *BDP-STRAT-01 (Sovereign Strategic Pivot)* and *BDP-GROW-01 (Price-Volume Strategic Balancing)*, which mandate the calculation of specific customer acquisition cost frictions.  
The platform actively rejects **Democratic Group Consensus Seeking** as a valid pattern. The BoardroomVoiceAgent enforces an "Anti-Consensus Preference Optimization" philosophy3. Corporate boards hold fiduciary duties of independent care and loyalty; consensus-chasing patterns systematically dilute risk visibility, prioritize social harmony over factual truth, and ultimately result in sub-optimal capital allocation3.  
Finally, the concept of **Total Automated Workflow Execution** is categorically excluded because it fundamentally violates the principle of *Human Augmentation Integrity*3. Under no circumstances should artificial intelligence be granted autonomous authority to finalize high-stakes, binding corporate board decisions. The agent acts strictly as an epistemic scaffolding layer designed to prepare the human executive. Legal, physical, and moral accountability remains entirely with the human sovereign entity3.
